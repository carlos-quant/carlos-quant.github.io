Financial regulation is written in natural language, but it governs behavior
that is almost entirely quantitative: capital ratios, exposure limits,
probability of default, value at risk. A regulator, a compliance officer, or
a litigator who can only read the text of a rule &mdash; and not the numbers
the rule is trying to constrain &mdash; is working with half the picture.

This is the starting premise of this site: that mathematics is not a
supporting tool for financial law, but part of its native language.

## Two examples

Consider a capital adequacy requirement. The legal text sets a ratio &mdash;
say, a minimum percentage of risk-weighted assets that must be held as
capital. Understanding whether a bank is compliant, and how it might adjust
its portfolio to stay compliant, is a constrained optimization problem
before it is anything else.

Or consider disclosure law in securities regulation. Courts often have to
decide whether a given fact was "material" &mdash; whether a reasonable
investor would have wanted to know it. Increasingly, that question gets
tested against actual market reaction: event studies, regression models
estimating abnormal returns around a disclosure date.

## What this means for how we should read the law

None of this argues that legal reasoning should be replaced by formulas.
Law is about legitimacy, fairness, and institutional design in ways that
resist full formalization, and it should stay that way. But it does argue
for a specific kind of literacy: the ability to move fluently between a
regulatory text and the mathematical structure it is trying to describe.

That's the project of this site &mdash; short pieces working through
specific legal and regulatory questions with whatever quantitative tools
actually clarify them, no more and no less.
